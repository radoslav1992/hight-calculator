Wondering how tall a child will be is natural. It is also a question that numbers can only answer as a range. Adult height reflects inherited potential, current growth, the timing of puberty, health, and ordinary biological variation. No home calculator can see all of those factors.

The most useful prediction is therefore not a single impressive number. It is a **center estimate, a realistic range, and an explanation of the inputs behind both**.

## Start with the genetic target

A common first estimate is the mid-parental, or Tanner, target. Add the biological parents' heights, include a 13 cm sex adjustment, and divide by two:

- For a boy: `(mother's height + father's height + 13 cm) ÷ 2`
- For a girl: `(mother's height + father's height − 13 cm) ÷ 2`

This gives a midpoint rather than a promise. A conventional band of about 8.5 cm on either side reflects how broadly children from the same parent-height combination can vary.

Parent height matters because stature is strongly heritable, but inheritance is not a simple average. A child receives a different mix of genetic variants from each parent. Grandparents and the wider family can help explain why siblings with the same parents finish at different heights.

## Add today's height, weight, and age

The Khamis–Roche method adds more information. It combines age, biological sex, current stature, current weight, and average parent height with age-specific regression coefficients. It was designed to predict adult stature without a bone-age X-ray.

That makes it useful for an educational home estimate, but its original development sample matters. The [1994 Pediatrics paper indexed by PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/7936860/) describes a model developed from white American children without conditions that alter growth. Results should not be treated as equally validated for every child or population.

Measurements also carry error. A small difference in posture, time of day, shoes, flooring, or the way height is read can change an input. For a repeatable check, use a flat wall and floor, remove shoes, look straight ahead, and measure at a similar time of day.

## Understand the growth-curve view

A growth percentile compares a measurement with a reference population of the same age and sex. The CDC stature-for-age reference covers ages 2 to 20 and publishes the LMS values needed to calculate z-scores and percentiles.

Tallwise uses those [official CDC growth-chart data files](https://www.cdc.gov/growthcharts/cdc-data-files.htm) to locate the current stature on the curve. It then shows what the same standardized position would look like at age 20. This is a trajectory illustration—not a CDC-endorsed adult-height prediction.

One percentile is not a diagnosis. The pattern across repeated, accurately measured visits is more informative than one point. A clinician can interpret that pattern alongside family history, puberty, health, and previous records.

## Puberty changes the timing

Two children can reach a similar adult height on very different schedules. An early maturer may be taller than classmates for a time and then stop growing sooner. A late maturer may look shorter during early adolescence and continue growing later.

That is why a calculator should not simply add a standard number of centimeters remaining at each birthday. Puberty stage and recent growth velocity matter, but they are difficult to identify precisely without a clinical history. Tallwise allows a small contextual adjustment and deliberately caps it so a subjective answer cannot dominate the measured inputs.

## Sleep and nutrition support growth—they do not guarantee extra height

Adequate sleep and balanced nutrition support normal development. Severe or prolonged deficiencies can interfere with health and growth. But once needs are met, more protein, calcium, supplements, or sleep cannot reliably push a child beyond their genetic potential.

Be skeptical of products that promise to make a healthy child taller. A varied diet, age-appropriate movement, and consistent sleep are worthwhile for overall health regardless of final height.

## Read a prediction range responsibly

Treat the midpoint as the model's best central estimate. Treat the band as a reminder that individual outcomes vary. A range can widen when the three Tallwise methods disagree or when the child is younger, because more growth and more uncertainty remain.

The range is not a statistical guarantee for a specific child. It does not account for bone age, diagnosed conditions, medication effects, or a full longitudinal growth history.

> The best use of a height prediction is curiosity and planning—not judgment, comparison, or pressure.

## When to talk with a health professional

Ask a pediatrician or another qualified clinician about a child's growth when the concern is persistent, when measurements cross established percentile lines over time, or when growth seems unexpectedly fast or slow. Bring dated measurements if possible. A professional can check measurement quality, growth velocity, puberty timing, nutrition, family pattern, and whether any further assessment is appropriate.

Tallwise is designed to make the math transparent. It cannot tell you whether a child's growth is healthy, and it should never delay a check-up.
