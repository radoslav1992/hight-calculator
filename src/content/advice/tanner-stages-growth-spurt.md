Puberty changes the pace of growth dramatically. A child who is shorter than peers before a later growth spurt may close part of that gap. Another child may grow quickly earlier and finish sooner. Comparing them at one birthday can be misleading.

Tanner stages are one way clinicians describe this developmental progression. They are useful in context, but they are not a quiz score for families to diagnose at home.

## The five-stage framework

The framework runs from stage 1, before visible pubertal development, through stage 5, mature physical development. It describes changes in secondary sexual characteristics, not height alone.

Broadly:

1. **Stage 1:** prepubertal development.
2. **Stage 2:** early physical changes begin.
3. **Stage 3:** development advances and the growth spurt is often active.
4. **Stage 4:** changes continue while height velocity often begins to slow.
5. **Stage 5:** mature development; little linear growth typically remains.

The timing and combination of signs vary. A clinician's assessment is more nuanced than selecting the closest description.

## Peak height velocity

Peak height velocity means the fastest rate of linear growth during the adolescent spurt. It is a rate—usually understood from measurements across time—not a single height.

To estimate growth velocity, subtract an earlier accurate height from a later one and account for the time between them. Measurements should be far enough apart to reduce the effect of ordinary measurement noise. A few weeks is usually too short to tell a meaningful trend at home.

## Why timing affects predictions

Imagine two children who are both 13 years old and 155 cm tall. One is early in puberty and the other is nearing the end. The same current height does not imply the same amount of growth remaining.

Age-specific models partly capture the average pattern, but they cannot know an individual's biological maturity from chronological age alone. Bone-age assessment can add information in clinical settings when a professional considers it appropriate.

## Early and late maturation can both be normal

Families often inherit patterns of maturation. Asking when parents and siblings experienced their main growth spurts can provide context, although memories are imperfect.

Being early or late compared with classmates is not automatically a disorder. The question is whether the child's overall development and growth trajectory fit a healthy pattern. That judgment belongs with a qualified health professional.

## Avoid turning puberty into a comparison contest

Children and teens may already feel watched or judged as their bodies change. Repeated comments about being short, tall, early, or late can add pressure. Use neutral language, respect privacy, and focus on health rather than a desired final number.

A prediction should never be used to select a sport, set expectations about attractiveness, or encourage unproven growth products.

## What to track instead

A simple, respectful record can be useful:

- Date and standing height measured in a consistent way
- Weight measured without trying to manipulate the prediction
- Relevant health changes or medication discussed with a clinician
- The child's growth chart at routine visits

## When to seek advice

Bring concerns to a pediatrician or another qualified clinician when puberty seems unusually early or delayed, when growth velocity changes unexpectedly, or when the pattern causes significant worry. A professional can evaluate history, measurements, family pattern, and development together.

Do not use a web page to assign a clinical Tanner stage. The useful takeaway is simpler: **puberty timing changes how much growth may remain, and age alone cannot describe that timing perfectly**.
